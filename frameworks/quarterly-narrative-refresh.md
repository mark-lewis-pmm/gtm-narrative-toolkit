# Quarterly Narrative Refresh

Your narrative needs to evolve with your market. GTM Partners' research shows most GTM plans fail by Q2 because the plan from Q4 no longer matches the business by spring. This process keeps your narrative current without starting over every quarter.

---

## When to run this

Every 90 days. Block 2 hours. Involve your CMO, head of sales, head of product, and head of CS. Nobody else. Keep it small and honest.

---

## The process

### Step 1: Reality check (30 min)

Answer these questions as a group. No slides. No prep. Just answers.

- **What changed in our market in the last 90 days?** New competitors, customer behavior shifts, macro trends, regulatory changes.
- **What did we learn from customers that surprised us?** Things sales heard. Things CS heard. Things product heard from usage data.
- **Where did the narrative break?** Where did someone on the team struggle to tell the story? Where did a prospect not connect with it? Where did an internal team go off-script?
- **What are we saying that's no longer true?** Market claims that aged out. Customer pain points that shifted. Competitive positioning that needs updating.

### Step 2: Narrative stress test (30 min)

Pull up the current Shared Narrative Canvas. Read each of the five answers out loud. For each one, ask:

- [ ] Is this still true?
- [ ] Is this still the most important version of this truth?
- [ ] Can sales still use this language in a call today?
- [ ] Would a new hire understand this without additional context?

Mark anything that fails the test. Don't try to fix it in the room yet.

### Step 3: Rewrite what's broken (30 min)

Only rewrite the sections that failed the stress test. Don't touch what's working. Resist the urge to "refresh" language that's already landing.

Rules for rewriting:
- Keep the same structure (five questions, five answers)
- Use customer language, not marketing language
- If you can't agree on a rewrite in the room, the disagreement is the insight. Dig into it.

### Step 4: Pressure test with sales (following week)

Take the updated narrative to 2-3 senior sales reps. Not a presentation. A conversation.

Ask them:
- "Does this match what you're hearing from prospects?"
- "Would you use this language in a call?"
- "What's missing?"

Incorporate their feedback. They are your early warning system.

### Step 5: Cascade (within 2 weeks)

Update these artifacts to reflect the refreshed narrative:
- [ ] Sales pitch deck
- [ ] Website homepage and product pages
- [ ] Internal onboarding materials
- [ ] Competitive battlecards
- [ ] Analyst briefing docs

Don't announce a "narrative refresh." Just update the materials. People notice when the story changes. They don't need a memo telling them it changed.

---

## Common traps

**Over-rotating on one deal or one customer.** One lost deal is an anecdote. Three lost deals with the same objection is a signal.

**Changing the narrative because leadership got bored.** Executives hear the story every day. Customers hear it once. The narrative isn't stale just because you're tired of it.

**Rewriting everything every quarter.** If you're rebuilding from scratch every 90 days, your narrative wasn't grounded in a real market shift. Go back to question one on the canvas.

**Skipping the sales pressure test.** The narrative exists to travel. If sales can't use it, it doesn't exist.
